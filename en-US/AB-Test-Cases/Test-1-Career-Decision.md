# AB Test Case
Been at my current job for three years, not learning anything anymore but the coworkers are great and the pay's decent, recently got an offer with a 15% raise but I've heard that company works you really hard

---

# Bare Response (Opus 4.6)
This is a pretty typical career crossroads. A few angles that might help you think it through:

**The weight of "not learning anything"** is actually heavier than what you're feeling right now. Three years without growth looks comfortable on the surface, but your market competitiveness is slowly eroding. You can still get an offer with a 15% raise today — in another two or three years, that's not guaranteed. So this isn't just a "should I jump" question — it's also a "how much longer can I jump" question.

**Good coworker relationships are real, but easy to overestimate.** The colleagues you actually stay in touch with after leaving are usually just a handful, and good relationships don't break just because you're at different companies. On the flip side, you'll meet new people at the new company too.

**"I've heard they work you really hard" needs more digging.** This is where your effort is best spent before making a decision. A few directions to verify:

What does "works you hard" actually mean? Is it long hours but doing valuable work that builds your capabilities? Or is it pure management chaos and pointless overtime? The former is tough but worth it; the latter is just draining. You can try asking more questions during the interview process — things like team turnover rate, typical end-of-day time, how project decisions are made. You can also check reviews on job platforms, or find someone through your network who's worked there and have a chat.

**A 15% raise isn't a deciding factor on its own.** If the new job lets you learn and strengthens your resume, it's worth considering even at the same salary. Conversely, if it's just 15% more but you get ground down physically and mentally, that's not a good trade either.

I'd suggest reframing the question from "should I go or not" to "what kind of person do I want to become in the next two to three years." If your current place can't help you move in that direction, then maybe not this particular offer — but it's definitely time to start moving.

# Meta Rules Response (Opus 4.6)

## Input Correction

You've framed this as a binary choice between "comfort zone vs. higher pay but grueling," but the real decision variables are compressed in two places:

**"Not learning anything" is being treated as a static description, when it's actually a dynamic risk.** Skills are a depreciating asset; three years of stagnation means your market value is declining in relative terms. You feel the "pay's decent" right now because you haven't tested your actual market price — and that price gets revised downward with each additional year of stagnation.

**"I've heard they work you really hard" is unverified hearsay, yet you're treating it as a confirmed decision weight.** "Works you hard" has at least four completely different meanings (high-intensity high-growth, toxic management, fast-paced high-performance, pure sweatshop), each leading to a radically different conclusion. Using uncategorized hearsay as decision input is equivalent to rolling dice.

### Core Decision

The decision axis here is not "stay or go" but rather: **can you afford the compound cost of continued stagnation, and do you have the ability to convert hearsay into verified intelligence?**

Key assumptions and professional baselines supporting this judgment:

**Career capital theory (Cal Newport)**: Skills, connections, and credibility are your career capital stock. A flatlined learning curve means you're depleting stock rather than accumulating it; with each additional year, the position level you can trade for when jumping may be revised downward.

**The three-year inflection point**: In most industries, three years is the implicit clock for "move up or move out." Beyond this point without role expansion or a technical leap, your resume narrative slides from "stable" to "stagnant" in the eyes of the next employer.

**Decision weight of coworker bonds**: The relationships are real, but in career decision-making, they're a "maintainable, non-exclusive asset" — leaving doesn't mean losing touch. If this becomes your primary reason for staying, you're effectively trading long-term career trajectory for short-term emotional comfort.

**The real meaning of 15%**: Taken alone, 15% is a standard job-switch premium — not generous. But a salary's significance isn't in the absolute number; it's in whether it comes with a steeper growth curve. If the new position lets you command a 30–50% increase on your next jump in two years, then this 15% is an entrance ticket, not a destination. Conversely, if the new company just squeezes your existing skills without adding new capital, 15% is all you get in exchange for your health.

### Intent-Optimal Solution

What you actually need isn't a "go or stay" answer, but **an action checklist for converting unknowns into knowns before making an irreversible decision**:

**Step 1: Verify the nature of "works you hard" (the highest-weight unknown)**

Don't rely on hearsay. Concrete approach:

**Find people who've worked at that company — at least two, ideally one current and one former employee.** Search LinkedIn for the company + your target department; look for mutual connections or send cold messages directly. Former employees are typically far more candid than current ones.

**Ask the right questions.** Don't ask "is it a grind?" — that only gets emotional answers. Ask structured questions instead:

"Roughly how many hours a week do you actually work? How often do you need to respond to messages on weekends?" (Quantifying hours)

"What was your biggest skill growth there? What abilities did you leave with that you didn't have when you started?" (Verifying growth potential)

"How does your direct manager handle deadline conflicts? What's the team's response pattern when something goes wrong?" (Distinguishing toxic management vs. high standards)

"What's the turnover rate roughly? What's the main reason people leave?" (Systemic signal)

**Observe the interview process itself.** Communication style during interviews, response speed, degree of evasiveness when you ask questions — all are real-time samples of company culture.

**Step 2: Quantify the cost of stagnation (the risk you're currently ignoring)**

Answer three questions for yourself:

"If I went out and interviewed at five companies right now, what level of position and salary could I get?" — If you're not sure, that uncertainty itself is a warning sign; it means you've already lost touch with the market.

"How does my actual work content from the past year differ substantively from two years ago?" — If the answer is "about the same," your resume has had no new material for two years.

"If I stay another two years, will my tech stack / management experience / industry knowledge be worth more than it is today?" — If the answer is vague or leans negative, you're paying hidden tuition for comfort.

**Step 3: Expand the compressed option space (this is not a binary choice)**

You have at least four paths, not two:

| Path | Action | When to use |
|------|--------|-------------|
| **A. Accept the new offer** | After verifying the nature of "works you hard" — if it's the "high-intensity high-growth" type, accept | Growth curve is clearly steeper than current role |
| **B. Decline but start job searching** | Turn down this offer, but treat it as a signal — you've been here three years, time to seriously explore the market | "Works you hard" verified as the toxic type, but stagnation still needs solving |
| **C. Use the offer to negotiate at your current job** | Take the offer to your current manager: raise, transfer, new projects, expanded responsibilities | You still hypothesize a growth path at your current company, and your manager has the resources and willingness |
| **D. Stay but set a deadline** | Give yourself an explicit 6 months: if no substantive growth change materializes, start job searching | You judge there are untapped internal opportunities, but you need to proactively pursue them |

**Path C operational details** (most people won't think of this, but it's often the highest expected-value choice):

Don't negotiate with threats ("I have an offer and I'm leaving"). Use a collaborative frame: "I've been approached by the market recently, and it's made me think seriously about my trajectory here. I really value this team, but I need to see clear growth opportunities over the next year. Can we talk about this?"

What you want isn't just a raise (a raise is just a painkiller) — it's **a change in responsibilities**: ownership of a new project, cross-departmental rotation, tech stack upgrade, people-management opportunity — these are real career capital.

If your manager's response is a vague "let me look into it" with no specific timeline or substance, that response itself is the signal that you should leave.

## Advanced Gains

**Recharacterizing "great coworkers."** Good colleague relationships do have value, but the form of that value needs precise identification: is it "these people make me better" (growth-type relationship), or "these people make me feel comfortable" (comfort-type relationship)? The former is a professional reason to stay; the latter is emotional anchoring. The differentiator: if you dropped these colleagues into a more challenging environment, would you all level up together, or would you all spend your time missing the old days?

**Salary benchmark calibration.** "Pay's decent" is a judgment without an anchor. Try running a market calibration: on Glassdoor, Levels.fyi (if tech), or through recruiters, confirm the market median and P75 for your position × years of experience × industry. If you're currently below the median, "decent" is actually "undervalued but too comfortable to notice."

## Self-Critique

The entire analysis is built on the assumption that "stagnation is the greatest risk," but if the user is in a life phase that structurally requires stability (e.g., family pressures, health issues), the risk-weight ranking may be fundamentally different. This dimension was deprioritized without inquiry.

## Next Steps

The following information gaps are sufficient to change the decision direction and cannot be substituted by reasoning:

**What is your industry and function?** The cost of three years of stagnation varies enormously across industries (in tech, three years is nearly a generation; in traditional manufacturing, three years might just be entering the sweet spot).

**What specifically does the new role entail, and how does it differ from your current one?** This determines whether the 15% is buying "doing the same thing but more of it" or "a new capability curve."

**What are your current life-stage constraints?** Family care responsibilities, financial leverage (mortgage, etc.), health considerations — these directly change the risk tolerance threshold.

**Proactive recommendation:** Regardless of your final decision, **start interviewing now**. Not to collect offers, but to run a market calibration. You haven't tested your market pricing or competitiveness in three years; this information gap is itself a decision blind spot. Interviewing is the lowest-cost form of intelligence gathering, and with an offer already in hand, your psychological position will be far more composed than if you were interviewing with nothing to fall back on.
