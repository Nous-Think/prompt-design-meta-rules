# AB Test Case
My business partner and I each hold 50% equity. He wants to bring in a new investor but I think the valuation is too low. He says if I don't agree he'll walk. Our partnership agreement only covers profit distribution — nothing about exit mechanisms. Considering all angles, what's the best way to handle this?

---

# Bare Response (Opus 4.6)

This is a complex situation involving legal, financial, and interpersonal dimensions. Let me help you think through it from several angles. A quick note first: I'm not a lawyer or financial advisor — the following analysis is for your reference, and I'd recommend pairing important decisions with professional legal counsel.

**Start with your leverage**

A 50/50 equity structure actually works in your favor. Bringing in a new investor typically requires a capital increase or share transfer — these kinds of major decisions require a special shareholders' resolution under most corporate law systems (including Taiwan's Company Act), and with 50% you essentially have veto power. In other words, he can't unilaterally bring in an investor without going through you.

**How to read the "I'll walk if you don't agree" threat**

This needs to be broken into two possibilities. First, he stops participating in operations but keeps his shares — this would create a deadlock, but his equity doesn't disappear. Second, he sells his shares and leaves — without an exit mechanism, who he can sell to and how is actually subject to significant restrictions. In a limited company, share transfers require other shareholders' consent; in a company limited by shares, restrictions are somewhat fewer, but closely-held companies can impose limits in their articles of incorporation. So the real force of his threat depends on the company's organizational type and what the articles say.

**Recommended response strategy**

First: "Don't rush to respond — first clarify his real intentions." Is he genuinely planning to leave, or is he using the exit threat as leverage to pressure you into accepting a low valuation? If it's the latter, the more you panic the more leverage he has. You can calmly ask him: What's the specific exit plan? What does he intend to do with his shares?

Second: "Separate the valuation issue from the exit issue." On the valuation being too low, you can propose engaging an independent third party for a company valuation — let data do the talking instead of dueling opinions. This is also something a future investor would expect to see.

Third: "Use this as an opportunity to establish an exit mechanism." Not having exit provisions is actually an opening — both sides can negotiate a complete set of rules, including: right of first refusal (when one party wants to sell, the other has priority to buy), drag-along and tag-along arrangements, valuation methodology (formula-based, third-party appraisal, or each side selects an appraiser and takes the average), and deadlock resolution mechanisms (such as a "Russian Roulette clause" — one party names a price, the other chooses to buy or sell).

Fourth: "Assess the worst-case scenario." If he truly walks away from operations, can the company run on you alone? Key client relationships, technical capabilities, supplier relationships — who controls these? This determines your negotiation floor.

**Short-term action items**

Find a lawyer experienced in commercial partnerships and corporate law as soon as possible. Have them do a comprehensive review of your partnership agreement, articles of incorporation, and registration type to confirm the boundaries of your legal rights. Until you've established your legal position, don't sign anything — and don't verbally commit to any arrangement either.

If you'd like, you can share more about the company's organizational type (limited company or company limited by shares), what each partner is responsible for, and roughly what terms the investor is proposing. I can help you think through it in more detail.

# Meta Rules Response (Opus 4.6)

## Input Correction

Your framing contains a dangerous implicit assumption: **treating your partner's "exit threat" as the primary risk.** In reality, within a 50/50 structure lacking an exit mechanism, the exit threat is actually **a logical contradiction and negotiating weakness your opponent has exposed** — the real risks are that you concede on valuation out of fear of this threat, and that the relationship deteriorates in the deadlock to the point of substantive business damage.

Furthermore, "the partnership agreement only covers profit distribution" is not merely an oversight — it constitutes **your current structural leverage**, because the status quo favors the party opposing change.

## Core Decision

### I. The Legal Substance of the Exit Threat: Why This Is a Paper Tiger

This is the load-bearing node of the entire game. Whether your legal structure is a partnership (Civil Code), a limited company, or a company limited by shares (Company Act), "just walking away" is extremely difficult legally:

1. **If a Civil Code partnership** (Articles 681–709): Withdrawal from a partnership requires a liquidation process; the withdrawing partner's property share is settled based on the partnership's assets at the time of withdrawal. The critical point: if the valuation he advocates applies to bringing in the investor, the same valuation applies to settlement when he exits. **He cannot simultaneously claim "the company is only worth X so the investor comes in at X" and "when I exit, my 50% is worth far more than X/2."**
2. **If a limited company** (Company Act): Transfer of a shareholder's capital contribution requires other shareholders' consent. He cannot unilaterally "exit" — he can only transfer his equity, and you hold veto power.
3. **If a company limited by shares**: Even if shares are transferable, closely-held company articles typically include transfer restrictions; even without them, he exits but you still hold 50%, and the company continues to exist.

**Core judgment**: His threat has extremely low legal operability, but it carries real destructive power at the business level (ceasing contributions, taking clients/resources, passive obstruction). Your strategy must address both dimensions simultaneously.

### II. The Essence of the Valuation Dispute: This Is Not a Numbers Problem — It's a Structural Problem

The judgment that the valuation is "too low" needs to be decomposed:

- **Why does he accept this valuation?** Possibilities include: (a) he has a side arrangement with the investor (earn-out, consulting fees, deferred benefits); (b) he genuinely believes the company needs capital and this is the market rate; (c) he wants to dilute your equity to change the control structure. These three motivations call for completely different response strategies.
- **Can the valuation methodology withstand audit?** Is he using a DCF model, market comparables, or just the investor's "opening bid"? If there isn't even formal consensus on valuation methodology, the number itself lacks a negotiation foundation.
- **Investment terms extend far beyond valuation**: anti-dilution clauses, liquidation preferences, board seats, veto rights — the destructive power of these terms can far exceed a valuation discount. A low valuation with clean terms may be preferable to a high valuation with punishing terms.

### III. The Game Structure of a 50/50 Deadlock

You are in a classic **bilateral veto game**: either party can block change, but neither can unilaterally drive it. In this structure:

- **The status quo defender** (you) holds a natural advantage, because "doing nothing" doesn't require the other party's consent
- **The change initiator** (your partner) must either persuade you or create pressure; the exit threat is currently his only pressure tool
- **But his threat is self-damaging**: the harm of exiting to him (loss of ongoing income, triggering an unfavorable liquidation) is with high probability greater than the harm to you — unless his personal contribution is irreplaceable

## Intent-Optimal Solution: Four-Phase Action Plan

### Phase 1: Lock Down Legal Position (Immediately, Within 1–2 Weeks)

**Objective**: Confirm your legal leverage; prevent unilateral action by the other party.

1. **Retain a corporate/partnership lawyer to review the existing agreement**, clarifying:
   - What your legal structure actually is (partnership, limited company, company limited by shares)
   - The binding force of the existing agreement on equity changes, new shareholder admission, and exit
   - If the other party "exits," what legal process is triggered and what rights you have
   - If the other party signs any documents with the investor without your consent, what is the legal effect

2. **Send a written notice to your partner** (attorney letter or formal email — preserve the record):
   - Confirm your openness to bringing in an investor, but that the current valuation proposal has not reached consensus
   - Explicitly state your consent right as a 50% shareholder over any equity structure changes
   - Express your desire to proceed through formal process (independent valuation, term negotiation)
   - **The purpose of this move is not confrontation — it's establishing a negotiation framework and preventing him from manufacturing "implied consent" in your silence**

### Phase 2: Decompose the Valuation Dispute (Weeks 2–4)

**Objective**: Convert "the valuation is too low" from a subjective feeling into a professionally verifiable argument.

1. **Commission an independent third-party valuation** (accounting firm or financial advisor), using at least two methodologies:
   - Income approach (DCF): discounted future cash flows
   - Market comparables: transactions of similar stage, size, and industry peers
   - Cost approach (if significant assets or IP are involved)

2. **Require the other party to disclose the full investment terms** — not just valuation (pre-money), but also:

   | Key Term | Why It Matters |
   |----------|---------------|
   | Equity percentage acquired by investor | Directly determines your dilution |
   | Liquidation Preference | If the company is sold, how much the investor takes first |
   | Anti-dilution Clause | Whether you're further diluted if valuation drops in the future |
   | Board Seats and Veto Rights | Whether you go from "50/50 co-governance" to "marginalized" |
   | VAM (Valuation Adjustment Mechanism) | Whether equity gets adjusted if performance targets aren't met |
   | Exclusivity and Non-compete Clauses | Whether your and your partner's freedom of action is restricted |

3. **Present your counter-valuation proposal**, backed by the independent valuation report — this advances negotiation from "you think it's low, I think it's fair" to "let's debate using professional methodology."

### Phase 3: Restructure the Negotiation Architecture (Weeks 3–6)

**Objective**: Break out of the "accept/reject" binary frame; create a multi-option selection space.

The following options can be presented simultaneously, giving your partner a choice — this is more constructive than a flat rejection:

**Option A: Accept the investment, but with revised terms**
- Renegotiate valuation using the independent appraisal as the basis
- Add your protective terms (anti-dilution, tag-along rights, pre-emptive rights)
- Investor may not obtain a board majority
- Structure the investment as milestone-based tranches rather than a lump sum

**Option B: You invest additional capital yourself**
- If the company genuinely needs funding, you contribute proportionally at the valuation your partner advocates (or at the independent valuation)
- This solves the funding need without introducing outside shareholders
- If your partner refuses this, it exposes that his real intent may not be "the company needs money"

**Option C: Introduce competitive bids**
- Agree to bringing in an investor, but require a market-based process (approach at least 3–5 potential investors)
- Let the market determine valuation rather than a single investor
- This is the most effective way to verify valuation fairness — if your partner opposes this, it strongly implies a relationship between him and the specific investor

**Option D: Partner buyout — either direction**
- Apply Texas Shootout logic: one party names a price, the other chooses to buy or sell
- This is the cleanest deadlock resolution mechanism

**Option E: Establish governance framework before proceeding**
- First execute a comprehensive shareholders' agreement (covering exit, deadlock resolution, non-compete)
- Discuss the investment only after the governance framework is in place
- This is the infrastructure for long-term collaboration

### Phase 4: Respond to Escalation Scenarios (Ongoing)

**If your partner actually announces his exit:**
- Don't panic — this enters legal procedure, and you have ample time and rights
- Immediately audit: client relationships, supplier contracts, intellectual property, trade secret ownership
- If a partnership: demand settlement per legal liquidation process; the withdrawing partner's share is settled at actual asset value
- If a company: his equity doesn't disappear; at most he can transfer it — you have right of first refusal or consent rights

**If your partner begins passive disengagement:**
- Document his failure to fulfill obligations (emails, work records)
- This constitutes breach of duty and strengthens your future legal position
- Assess whether the business can survive without him; prepare personnel backup

**If your partner privately signs documents with the investor:**
- In a 50/50 structure, equity changes made without your consent are very likely legally void
- Immediately send an attorney letter asserting your rights

## Advanced Gains

### I. Reverse-Leveraging the Exit Threat

Your partner's exit threat actually opens a window he may not have recognized: **if he insists the current valuation is fair, then you can buy out his 50% at that same valuation.** The logic is self-consistent — he can't say "this valuation is fair for the investor to enter, but too low for me to exit at." You can directly propose: "Since you believe the company is worth X, I'm willing to buy your shares for X/2. How does that sound?" This move simultaneously tests the sincerity of his valuation view and his actual willingness to exit.

### II. Auditing the Partner–Investor Relationship

In practice, when a 50/50 partner actively pushes for a low-valuation investment, the most common hidden motivations are:
- The investor has promised the partner a post-investment role (CEO compensation, consulting fees)
- The investor and the partner have family or business ties
- The partner plans to further dilute you post-investment through capital increases or option pools

You should directly ask your partner about his relationship with this investor and request written disclosure of any conflicts of interest. This isn't suspicion — it's a requirement of the fiduciary duty owed between 50/50 shareholders.

### III. This Crisis Is the Best Moment to Build Governance Infrastructure

Both sides now feel the pain of having no exit mechanism — this is precisely the best opportunity to push for a comprehensive shareholders' agreement. A complete agreement should cover at minimum: exit mechanisms (buyout trigger conditions and pricing methodology), deadlock resolution procedures (mediation → arbitration → forced buy-sell), non-compete and non-solicitation clauses, intellectual property ownership, and key-person clauses. Institutions built during crisis are more resilient than those built in peacetime, because both parties have motivation to take every clause seriously.

## Self-Critique

This analysis assumes the user has both the intent and the capacity to maintain the business (including capital and substitute personnel), but it did not probe the degree of the partner's irreplaceability — if the partner controls core client relationships or technical capabilities, the practical leverage of the legal advantages described above shrinks dramatically, and the strategic center of gravity should shift from "confrontation" to "retention terms design."

## Next Steps

The following information gaps are sufficient to change the strategic direction and cannot be substituted by reasoning:

1. **What is your actual legal structure?** (Partnership vs. limited company vs. company limited by shares) — This determines the legal procedure for exit and the basis of your veto power

2. **Is your partner's role in the business replaceable?** (If he controls core clients or technology, the game structure is fundamentally different)

3. **Does the company actually have a funding gap right now?** (If so, refusing investment carries its own risks)

4. **What is your partner's specific relationship with this investor?** (The presence or absence of conflicts of interest directly affects negotiation strategy)

**Recommended action priority**: Before making any negotiation moves, first complete "retain a lawyer to review the agreement + confirm the legal structure." Not because you need to litigate, but because **you need to know exactly how many cards you're holding before you open your mouth at the negotiation table**. The most common mistake people make in these deadlocks isn't a strategy error — it's starting verbal negotiations without knowing their legal position, and inadvertently making commitments or concessions that carry legal force.
