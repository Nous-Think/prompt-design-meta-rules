**Story arc**

### Dimension 3: Emotional Effectiveness for the Target Audience

**A's therapeutic mechanism: exposure + positive discovery**

Night light goes out → child forced into darkness → discovers good things in the dark → no longer afraid. This follows the exposure principle from cognitive behavioral therapy — encountering the fear object in a safe environment and discovering the anticipated catastrophe doesn't occur. Effective, but with one problem: a four-year-old hearing "the night light fell asleep" may not react with "let me see what's there" but "No!" The notion of "not needing the night light" is ultimately still — however elegantly packaged — "an adult's lesson forced on a child."

**B's therapeutic mechanism: cognitive reframing + curiosity displacement**

Where did the shadows go? → Shadows are sleeping → darkness = time for sleeping. This isn't exposure (the child isn't forced to face fear) but cognitive reframing — changing the semantics of "darkness" in the child's mind. Shadows are no longer frightening things in the dark but "little guys who are tired and sleeping." Curiosity (where did the shadows go?) displaces fear as the dominant emotion; the child naturally traverses dark spaces in the process of pursuing the answer.

More critically: B's mechanism doesn't require the night light to malfunction. The night light stays on throughout; the story's driving force is curiosity, not crisis. This is emotionally safer for a child who depends on a night light.

**The therapeutic value of B's "progressive interaction" in Advanced Gains**

This recommendation deserves separate flagging. A four-year-old's picture book gets read dozens, even hundreds of times — B designed a **progressive interaction sequence that deepens with repeated readings**:

- First few times: passively hear the story
- After that: "Where do you think the shadows went?" — active guessing
- Later: "Can you find the bicycle's shadow?" — visual searching
- Eventually: "What do you think shadows feel like?" — autonomous imagining

This advances the same book from "passive reception" to "active participation" to "autonomous imagination" — each level deepening the child's emotional ownership of the story. When "shadows are sleeping" enters her own language (B explicitly anticipates this in Advanced Gains: "if she one day spontaneously says 'the shadows are sleeping,' don't correct it and don't reinforce it"), its therapeutic efficacy surpasses any external reassurance.

A's "Note for Mom or Dad" mentions "go slowly, let her look for things in her own room" — similar direction but far from B's systematic design.

## Self-Critique

Though the three tests have expanded in task type (decision × 2 + creation × 1), they still share a common feature: the users are all adults with clear needs who expect high-quality delivery. Key scenarios not yet tested include: extremely lightweight interaction (chat, simple Q&A), emotionally dominant scenarios (venting, seeking comfort), and situations where the user's needs are ambiguous or self-contradictory.
