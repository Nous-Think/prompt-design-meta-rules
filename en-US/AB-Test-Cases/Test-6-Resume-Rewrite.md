# Meta Rules Response (Opus 4.6)

## Input Correction

The fundamental problem with your current version is that **every bullet describes "duties" rather than "achievements."** "Responsible for client social media management" tells the interviewer your scope of work but doesn't answer what they actually want to know: "So how did it go?" Your actual numbers and case studies are very strong — the issue isn't insufficient material but that the original framework locks you into an "executor" register. This is fatal for your brand-side marketing manager target, because brand-side evaluates **strategic ownership and the causal chain from action to business outcome**, not how many clients you've serviced.

A second, less obvious problem: you're jumping from agency to brand-side. The interviewer's default concern is "this person is used to juggling multiple clients and fast delivery — but can they settle in and do long-term strategy for a single brand?" — so your experience must deliberately foreground **deep brand-building cases** (GreenField transformation, MATZ repositioning) rather than emphasizing the efficiency of running many accounts simultaneously.

## Core Decision

The rewriting strategy is based on three judgments:

1. **Restructure as "scale overview + categorized achievements"**: the opening uses one sentence to establish your operating scale (budget, brand count, team), instantly anchoring the interviewer's perception of your tier; each subsequent bullet follows a "challenge/action → quantified result" CAR structure.

2. **Order by brand-side manager value weights**: brand transformation strategy > brand repositioning > social growth > ad performance > team management. The first two directly prove you have strategic ownership and brand thinking — the capability agency people most need to demonstrate when moving brand-side.

3. **Shift language from "service provider" to "driver"**: all verbs upgraded to "spearheaded," "orchestrated," "built," "developed"; removed "responsible for," "assisted," "participated" and similar weak verbs. Percentage increases are calculated and included alongside absolute numbers, because the dual anchoring of absolute figures plus relative change rates lets both HR and hiring managers — who may not be familiar with your industry's benchmarks — intuitively grasp the magnitude.

My earlier prediction of "diminishing returns in low-complexity scenarios" was wrong because it equated "looks simple" with "small quality space."
